# BPMN, honestly: what the standard contains, where it breaks down, and what it takes to build an editor for it

*BPMN is twenty-plus years old, an ISO standard, and the default answer whenever a product needs "let users draw their workflows." It's also bloated, routinely misused, and served by tooling with real strings attached. Having recently spent time proving out a BPMN editor in Angular on ngDiagram, I want to give the notation the honest rundown I wish I'd read before starting — what's actually in it, what people actually use, why so many diagrams are quietly broken, and what that means if you're the one building the editor.*

---

Business Process Model and Notation exists because of a translation problem. Business people describe processes in prose and slideware; engineers implement them in code and configuration; and somewhere between the two, meaning leaks. BPMN's founding promise — first at BPMI in the early 2000s, then under OMG, culminating in BPMN 2.0 in 2011 — was a single diagram language readable enough for the business side and precise enough to be executed by a workflow engine. One picture, two audiences, no leak.

That promise is why BPMN won. It's an ISO/IEC standard, it's the native language of execution engines like Camunda and Flowable, and it's the notation every process-mining and workflow vendor claims compatibility with. When someone in a planning meeting says "something like BPMN," they're not naming a preference; they're naming the default.

But the promise contains a tension that explains most of what's wrong with BPMN in practice: *readable* and *precise* pull in opposite directions. Make the notation expressive enough to capture real process semantics and it grows enormous; keep it small enough to read and it can't say what engines need said. BPMN 2.0 resolved this by choosing both — a huge vocabulary with a readable core — and twenty years of real-world usage have been the experiment on whether that works. The results are in, and they're interesting. Let's start with what's actually in the box.

## The vocabulary: a guided tour

BPMN 2.0 defines somewhere north of 85 graphical elements. They organize into a handful of families, and it's worth walking through them properly, because both the elegance and the bloat live in the details.

**Events** are things that *happen* — the circles. And they're not really one element; they're a matrix. One axis is *position in the flow*: a start event kicks a process off, an end event finishes a path, an intermediate event happens along the way, and a boundary event sits glued to the edge of an activity, waiting to interrupt it (or, in its non-interrupting variant, to spawn a parallel path without killing the host). The other axis is the *trigger*: none, message, timer, error, escalation, cancel, compensation, conditional, link, signal, terminate, and two "multiple" variants. Cross the axes and you get roughly sixty distinct event shapes, each with its own ring style (thin for start, double for intermediate, thick for end, dashed for non-interrupting) and inner glyph (envelope, clock, lightning bolt...). There's real design intelligence here — a timer boundary event on a task reads instantly as "deadline" once you know the grammar. But sixty shapes is sixty shapes. For circles.

**Activities** are the work — the rounded rectangles. A plain task is "something happens here"; typed tasks refine it: a user task needs a human, a service task calls a system, send and receive tasks handle messages, script tasks run code, business-rule tasks evaluate decisions, manual tasks happen entirely offline. On top of the type, activities take *markers*: a loop marker for repetition, parallel or sequential multi-instance markers for "do this N times," a compensation marker for undo logic. And then activities go recursive: a subprocess is a whole process folded into a single box, expandable inline or drilled into; a call activity references a reusable process defined elsewhere; event subprocesses, transactions, and ad-hoc subprocesses each add their own border style and semantics. Again: expressive, systematic, and a lot.

**Gateways** are the diamonds — where flow splits and merges, and where most of BPMN's actual semantic weight sits. The exclusive (XOR) gateway picks one outgoing path based on conditions. The parallel (AND) gateway takes all paths at once and, when merging, waits for all of them. The inclusive (OR) gateway takes *one or more* paths — and merging one correctly is notoriously subtle, because the join has to know how many tokens to expect. The event-based gateway routes by which external event fires first. The complex gateway is the standard admitting defeat and offering a free-text escape hatch. Crucially, every gateway is the same diamond with a different inner marker, and each one can be *diverging* (splitting) or *converging* (merging) — a distinction the diagram shows only through context, which will matter a great deal when we get to validation.

**Swimlanes** are the containers. A *pool* represents a participant — a company, a department, a system — and, in the standard's eyes, owns an entire process. *Lanes* subdivide a pool by role or responsibility. A collapsed "black-box" pool represents an external party whose internals you don't know or don't care about — you see the messages going in and out, nothing more. The rules that come with containment are among BPMN's most characteristic: sequence flow may cross lanes but never a pool boundary; communication between pools happens exclusively by message flow. Two pools exchanging messages is no longer a plain process diagram but a *collaboration* — one of three additional diagram types BPMN 2.0 defines, alongside choreography (diagrams centered on the message exchange itself) and conversation (a high-level communication overview). Most people never touch those last two; many have never heard of them.

**Connecting objects** are the arrows, and they are not interchangeable. The solid arrow is sequence flow — control, the order of work. The dashed arrow with the open arrowhead is message flow — communication across pools. The dotted line is an association, tying annotations to elements. Sequence flows themselves have variants: a *conditional* flow carries an expression and a little diamond at its source; a *default* flow carries a slash and fires when nothing else does. Data associations — dotted, directed — connect activities to the data world.

**Data and artifacts** round it out: data objects (the page with the folded corner) for documents and payloads, data stores (the cylinder) for persistence, text annotations for comments, and visual groups — dashed boxes that gather elements *without* affecting flow or ownership, decoration only.

Step back and the design philosophy is visible: a small set of base shapes (circle, rectangle, diamond, arrow) systematically extended by borders, markers, and glyphs. As a piece of language design, it's genuinely good. The question is whether anyone can speak it.

## What people actually use

Here's where two decades of empirical research get delightfully blunt. Studies of large real-world model collections keep finding the same shape: **fewer than 20% of BPMN's vocabulary appears in practice at all.** The average diagram uses about nine distinct constructs. Only four or five — task, sequence flow, exclusive gateway, start and end events — are common across most models. Element usage follows a Zipf-like distribution: a tiny head does almost all the work, and a long tail of specialized shapes (compensation events, complex gateways, ad-hoc subprocesses, the entire choreography diagram type) shows up somewhere between rarely and never.

Roughly a third of diagrams use pools and lanes. A meaningful minority use message flows and boundary events. And then there's everything else, existing mostly in the spec, vendor documentation, and certification exams.

This isn't an indictment — every rich language has a core and a periphery, and the periphery earns its keep in the niches that need it. But it has a very practical consequence for anyone building or choosing a BPMN tool: **the "complete" palette with fifty icons is not a feature.** It's the wall a new user bounces off. The tools people find pleasant are the ones that lead with the nine things everyone uses and tuck the rest behind progressive disclosure — a properties panel, a type-picker, a search. When I scoped my own proof of concept, deciding to start from that small everyday core — and treat everything else as data to layer in later — was the decision that made the rest of the build feel tractable.

## The dirty secret: most process models are broken

Now for the part that deserves more airtime than it gets. BPMN's precision half — the reason it can be executed — means a diagram can be *wrong* in a way a sketch never can. Not ugly-wrong. Semantically wrong: describing a process that deadlocks, loses work, or contains steps that can never run.

And this isn't a theoretical concern. In one analysis of 585 industrial process models — real models, from real organizations — **22% contained deadlocks and 42% had faulty merges.** Think about that base rate. Nearly half.

The classic mistake is depressingly simple: split the flow with a parallel gateway (both branches run), then merge it with an exclusive gateway, or with no gateway at all. Now two tokens arrive where one was expected — work executes twice, or a join waits forever for a branch that already passed. The inclusive gateway's merge semantics are subtle enough that even experienced modelers get them wrong. Concurrency is hard, and BPMN hands it to people whose job is process analysis, not distributed systems.

Why don't tools catch this? Because validation turns out to be a staircase, and each step up costs roughly ten times the one below:

- **Schema checks** — is the XML well-formed against the spec? Table stakes, and semantically nearly worthless.
- **Connection rules** — may this element link to that one? The spec defines these as concrete tables (message flows must cross pools; a start event has no incoming sequence flow; an end event can't send). It boils down to a couple dozen deterministic rules. Cheap, and every serious tool should have them.
- **Structural checks** — whole-graph properties: a start and an end exist, nothing is disconnected, no task quietly splits flow without a gateway, no parallel-split-into-exclusive-join. This is where linters like bpmnlint live, with rule sets of around 25 checks. Notably, a *few* of these — especially the AND-split/XOR-join pattern — catch a disproportionate share of real deadlocks with no deep analysis at all.
- **Behavioral soundness** — the real thing: proving every process instance can complete, no tokens are left stranded, every activity is reachable. This requires mapping the diagram to a formal model (typically Petri nets) and exploring its state space. It's an active research area. The current frontier — look up *BPMN Analyzer 2.0* — checks models with thousands of elements in under half a second, and it represents years of specialist work.

The economics are lopsided: the first two useful tiers deliver maybe 80% of the "it catches my mistakes" value for 20% of the cost. But here's the sharper problem, and in my view the real gap in the BPMN tool market: even the tools that *do* detect deadlocks tend to report them as state-space counterexamples — a trace of markings and transitions, technically correct and completely useless to the analyst who just wants to know *which diamond to fix*. Detection is a solved-ish problem. Explanation — a red dot on the offending gateway, a plain-language sentence, a one-click fix — mostly doesn't exist. Whoever builds that isn't competing with the standard; they're completing it.

## The tooling tax

Which brings us to the tools, because BPMN's problems in practice are inseparable from what you're allowed to build with.

Search "BPMN library" and you land on [bpmn-js](https://bpmn.io/), the open-source engine behind Camunda's modeler and the de facto default. Credit where due: it reads and writes real BPMN 2.0 XML, its rendering is faithful, and for "load a .bpmn file, let users edit it, save valid XML" it's the fastest path that exists. But it comes with strings. The license requires the "powered by bpmn.io" watermark on every rendered diagram — the code displaying it may not be removed or modified, and requests for a commercial exception have, as far as I can tell, never been granted. For a white-labeled product or an enterprise that cares about branding, that's a hard conversation. Deep customization is the other string: the extension model handles shallow tweaks fine and fights you on anything structural, with thin documentation and no paid support tier to escalate to.

The commercial general-purpose canvases — GoJS, JointJS — are mature and powerful, with BPMN samples or paid BPMN shape packs, per-seat or per-project licensing, and their own shape models to learn. And everything in this paragraph and the last is framework-agnostic, which in an Angular or React application translates to "you wrap it": bridging someone else's event system and rendering model into your framework's world, forever maintaining the seam.

Then there's interchange, BPMN's quiet embarrassment. The 2.0 spec standardized not just semantics but diagram geometry (the BPMN DI section), precisely so models could move between tools. In practice, round-tripping a diagram through two vendors' tools has long been a lottery of dropped attributes, mangled layout, and vendor extension elements. It's better than it was; it's not the interoperability the standard promised.

## So what do you actually need to build one?

Here's the reframe that prototyping an editor forced on me, and that I'd offer anyone facing the same task: **decide which half of BPMN is actually yours.**

If your product needs the *standard* half — import `.bpmn` files, stay XML-compatible, hand models to an execution engine — then swallow the tooling tax and use a dedicated BPMN library. That's the problem it solves, and it solves it well.

But a large class of products needs something subtly different: a process editor that lives *inside* the product, matches its design system, speaks a curated subset of the notation, and enforces the product's own rules. For that job, the BPMN-specific library matters less than the *diagramming foundation* under it — because you're going to own the semantics either way, and what you need from a library is a canvas that makes owning them cheap.

That's the reasoning that led me to [ngDiagram](https://www.ngdiagram.dev/), an Apache-2.0, Angular-native diagramming library. My proof of concept was deliberately modest — the everyday BPMN core, swimlanes, auto-layout — but it was enough to test the fit against everything above, and the pattern that emerged is what matters:

**The vocabulary problem becomes a data problem.** In ngDiagram, every diagram element renders through a plain Angular component you write — so BPMN's entire shape system (rings, markers, glyphs on shared base shapes) collapses into a handful of components branching on data. An entire working subset fit comfortably in two. And that's precisely what makes the long tail unthreatening: event triggers, task markers, more gateways are new data fields and new template branches, not new integrations with someone's shape DSL. The progressive-palette design the usage research demands is just... a design decision you make, because the palette is yours.

**The container problem maps onto a real group model.** Pools and lanes are the point where flat node-and-edge libraries fall over, because containment is a system of constraints — lanes stack without gaps, share a width, never shrink below their contents, adopt whatever's dropped into them — that must keep holding while a user drags things around. ngDiagram has first-class groups: membership, drag-in/drag-out, containment rules, minimum-size hooks. The lane-stacking and resize-to-fit behavior still had to be written — but it was pure business rules, written in an afternoon or two, with the rendering layer never once in the way. Getting swimlanes to *behave* in a quick proof of concept was the moment I stopped worrying about the approach; that's usually the part where prototypes die.

**The spaghetti problem stays under your control.** ngDiagram ships no auto-layout, which sounds like a gap and is actually the point: BPMN layout is *domain-scoped*. Only sequence flows should drive it — feed message flows into a layered layout and unrelated pools drag toward each other. It should run per container, left to right, orthogonally. Pairing the library with elkjs and encoding exactly those rules took a fraction of the prototype's time — and it's the kind of rule a built-in generic "arrange" button could never have known. Layout runs when the user asks, never as a reflex under their cursor.

**The tax disappears.** Apache 2.0, no watermark, no per-seat fees, no wrapper around a foreign event system. The editor looks like the product because it's built from the product's own materials — same components, same design tokens, same theme switch.

The honest ledger has a debit side, and it's exactly what you'd expect: no BPMN XML, no built-in BPMN semantics, no validation engine. The standard's half of the problem is entirely on you — a proof of concept demonstrates the canvas is a non-issue, not that the semantics come for free. For an embedded, branded, subset-speaking editor, that's not a bug in the plan — it *is* the plan — but you should walk in knowing it.

## A drawing is not a process

If I could leave you with one idea, it's the distinction that all of BPMN's troubles — the bloat, the broken models, the validation gap — ultimately trace back to: the difference between a *drawing* of a process and a *model* of one.

A drawing has shapes and arrows. A model knows things: that this gateway diverges and that one converges, that this event fires on a timer, that this flow carries a condition and that one is the default, that this lane is a participant that owns a process. Every valuable thing BPMN promises — execution, verification, unambiguous communication — lives in that knowledge, not in the picture. And most process diagrams in the wild, including plenty made in expensive tools, are drawings wearing a model's clothes. The 22%-deadlock statistic is what that looks like at scale.

So whether you're choosing a tool, building one, or just modeling: put your effort where the semantics are. Use the nine elements everyone understands rather than the sixty nobody does. Prefer an editor that can tell you *which diamond is wrong* over one with a bigger palette. And if you're building the editor yourself, pick the foundation that lets you spend your time on the model — because the shapes, it turns out, are the easy 20%.

---

*The opinions above come from a working proof of concept — an Angular app built on ngDiagram that exercises the everyday BPMN core: swimlanes that behave, domain-scoped auto-layout, an editor that looks like the product it would live in. It's a demonstration of what the approach makes possible, not a finished editor — the semantic model and the validation story sketched above are exactly where such a build would go next, and probably worth an article of their own.*
